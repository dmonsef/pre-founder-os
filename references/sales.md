# Founder-Led Sales

The founder has an MVP. Now they need customers. The instinct is to run ads, hire a marketing consultant, or build a "growth engine." Don't. In the early days, founder-led sales is the most important thing.

## Do things that don't scale

Paul Graham wrote the definitive essay on this. The most successful YC companies got their first users through unscalable, manual, often embarrassing hustle. Airbnb founders went door to door in New York. Stripe founders installed their product for early users on the spot. DoorDash founders personally delivered food.

The founder does not need a marketing strategy to get their first 100 customers. They need to talk to people directly and convince them one at a time.

The things that feel too manual, too slow, too unscalable — those are exactly the things they should be doing right now.

## Why founders must do sales first

Founder-led sales serves two purposes:

1. **It saves capital.** Every dollar spent on ads or consultants before understanding the customer is wasted. The founder is paying to learn things they could learn for free by talking to people.
2. **It creates the highest-fidelity feedback loop.** Doing sales themselves, the founder hears directly where people lose interest, what blocks them from paying, what words resonate, and what falls flat. No report or dashboard gives this.

Learning cannot be outsourced. A marketing consultant optimizes for metrics. The founder needs to optimize for understanding. Those are different things.

## Go where customers are

Customers are somewhere. They hang out in specific places, online and offline. The founder's job is to find those places and show up.

- **Reddit communities** for niche interests and problems
- **Facebook groups** for specific topics
- **Slack and Discord communities** for professional niches
- **Twitter/X** conversations around specific keywords
- **LinkedIn** for B2B prospects
- **Local meetups** where their people gather
- **Industry conferences** (but only if buyers are there)

Find the watering holes. Go there. Listen first. Then engage.

## Don't spam. Engage.

When the founder finds a community, do not immediately post about the product. That's spam. They'll get banned and burn the opportunity.

Instead:

- Answer questions. Be genuinely helpful.
- Share insights from experience.
- Ask thoughtful questions about the problems being solved.
- Build relationships before pitching anything.

When the founder has credibility in a community, people will ask what they're working on. That's when they say.

If getting banned from communities, they're doing it wrong. Slow down. Add value first.

## The first 100 customers

No marketing automation needed. Just persistence and a spreadsheet.

- Make a list of people who might have the problem
- Reach out personally (email, DM, LinkedIn, Twitter)
- Have real conversations
- Ask if they want to try the product
- Follow up relentlessly

It's slow. It's manual. It works. And the founder will learn more from 100 direct conversations than from 10,000 ad impressions.

## Learn who the customer is

With the first 100 customers through direct sales, pay attention to patterns:

- Who are they? (job title, company size, industry)
- Where did the founder find them?
- What words resonated?
- What objections did they have?
- Why did they say yes?

From these patterns, define a persona. Not a made-up marketing persona. A real one based on actual humans who paid money.

The persona should be specific enough that the founder could text them. "Marketing managers at Series A startups who are frustrated with their CRM" is good. "Professionals aged 25-45" is useless.

## Building the marketing engine

Once the founder knows who the customer is and where they hang out, they can start building repeatable channels:

1. **Find more places where those people are.** If the first 100 came from Reddit, what other subreddits? If LinkedIn, what content resonates?
2. **Create content that speaks to them.** Write about their problems. Use their words. Share stories from early customers.
3. **Build systems to do what the founder did manually.** If customers came from answering questions on Twitter, can that be done more systematically?

This is how a marketing engine is born. Not from a consultant's playbook. From patterns discovered by doing the work.

## Never stop talking to customers

Even with a marketing engine, even with a sales team, even with thousands of customers — the founder should still be talking to customers directly.

This is what the best founders do for the entire life of their company. It keeps them connected to real problems. It gives direct feedback on the product. It prevents disconnection from the people they serve.

Founders who stop talking to customers start building features nobody wants.

## When to spend money on marketing

The founder earns the right to spend money when:

1. They know exactly who their customer is
2. They know where the customer hangs out
3. They know what message resonates
4. They have a product the customer will pay for
5. They have a repeatable sales process

Until then, spending money is just guessing with cash. Do the manual work first.
