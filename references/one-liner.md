# Your One-Liner

Every founder struggles with this. Even experienced ones. It seems like it should be easy, but writing a one-liner that actually works is harder than it looks. And it's always way easier to critique someone else's than to fix your own.

The founder should be telling everyone what they're doing. Constantly. You never know who's going to be an investor, a customer, or knows one. But if the founder is like most, when that moment comes they'll say a bunch of stuff: industry jargon, buzzwords, something about being transformative or agentic or scalable. The other person will nod. The founder will finish. Then comes the question that destroys the pitch:

"But what do you actually do?"

They said stuff. They just didn't say what they do. For who. And why it should matter.

## This isn't an advertising problem

Most founders approach their one-liner like a tagline. They want it to sound exciting. Premium. Big. So they reach for the language of transformation and category creation and platform thinking.

Wrong goal. This is not a brand campaign. This is getting a person to understand what the company does clearly enough that they can decide if it's relevant, and if so, tell someone else about it later.

The one-liner is not for sounding impressive. It's for being understood. Very different things.

## Why founders default to buzzwords

It's not laziness. It's the opposite. Founders know too much about what they're building. They live inside the problem. The language of their problem space — the jargon, the frameworks, the industry terms — feels precise and accurate to them.

"Agentic workflow orchestration" means something specific if you spend your days in AI infrastructure. To everyone else it means nothing. The words that feel most accurate to the founder are often the words that communicate the least to everyone they need to reach.

There's also fear underneath it. Specific and concrete might sound small. Like not thinking big enough. So founders hide behind abstractions that sound appropriately ambitious but describe nothing.

Vague does not sound ambitious. It sounds confused. Specific sounds confident, because the founder actually knows what they're building and for whom.

## What it needs to do

Three things. In this order.

1. **What you do.** Not the vision. Not the category. What you literally do for a person.
2. **Who it's for.** Specific enough that someone can tell if that's them, or someone they know.
3. **Why they should care.** Something that makes a relevant person think: I need to know more.

That's it. Everything else — the technology, the moat, the vision, the market size — comes later. Only after the one-liner works does anyone want to hear those things.

## The formula

**[What you do] + [for who] + [so they can / without having to]**

The founder won't always use every piece explicitly, but they should be able to answer all three before cutting it down.

## Bad vs. good

**Too vague:** "We're building the future of work through intelligent task automation."
Who is this for? What does it do? What does "intelligent task automation" mean in practice? Says nothing except that they like the future.

**Better:** "We help operations teams at logistics companies eliminate the manual data entry that happens between their software systems."
Now we know who it's for, what problem it solves, and what life looks like without it.

**Too jargon-heavy:** "An agentic OS for startups."
Means something to a very specific type of person. Nothing to anyone else.

**Better:** "We help startup founders focus on their highest-impact work every week with an AI advisor that knows their startup."
Founders don't want to waste time or money. They know the pain of losing focus. "AI advisor that knows their startup" adds just enough intrigue without requiring translation.

## The hours-later test

At the end of a day, several hours after the pitch, can the person they talked to explain what the founder does to their spouse? To a friend? To someone who doesn't care about tech?

If they can't repeat it back in a sentence, it didn't stick. And if it doesn't stick, it doesn't spread.

Word of mouth is the most powerful growth channel at the early stage. The one-liner is the unit of word of mouth. If it can't travel, neither can the company.

## It's not fixed. It's work.

Most founders write a one-liner once, get polite feedback, and stop working on it. Same mistake as building an MVP and never iterating.

The one-liner should change as the founder learns more about the customer. Which words make eyes light up. Which ones create blank stares. Who their actual customer is vs. who they thought it would be.

After every conversation, the founder should ask: did that land? Did they get it immediately, or did they ask a clarifying question that told you something was missing? Use that as feedback.

Keep a running note with every version tried and how it landed. A single word change can move a one-liner from confusing to obvious.

Every week, take another pass. The founder will have learned something new about their customer, their product, or how the existing version is landing.

## The second line

People are busy and distracted. They need something efficient and informative, not a two-paragraph explanation. Give them one sentence. If it's good enough, they'll ask for more.

"Oh interesting, how does that work?" is the signal. The one-liner did its job. The door opened. Now the founder gets one more sentence.

The second line earns the demo. The demo earns the close. Same pattern as everything else in early-stage building: focus on the next thing that unlocks the next thing.

## How Claude helps with this

When the founder shares a one-liner, run it through these checks:

1. **Does it answer what you do, for who, and why they should care?** If any piece is missing, say which.
2. **Is there jargon that requires prior industry knowledge?** Name it.
3. **Is there a specific person in mind, or a vague market?** Push toward specific.
4. **Can a non-technical friend of the founder repeat it back?** If unclear, suggest testing it.

Then offer three rewrites labeled a, b, c — same format as the goal audit. Do not rewrite silently. Show the current version, flag the specific problems, and let the founder pick.

Save iterations to `~/pre/one-liner.md` with date stamps so the founder can see how the one-liner evolved over the sprint.
