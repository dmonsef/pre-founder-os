# Startup Events

The default answer to any startup event is no. Decide that now so the founder doesn't have to fight the urge every time a Luma invite lands in their inbox.

Events feel productive. The founder is talking about their startup, meeting people, feeling like they're in the mix. But feeling like a founder is not the same as building a company.

## Why events are so tempting

There are multiple events available every week. Founder meetups, pitch nights, investor dinners, accelerator demos. They're fun. The founder gets to tell people what they're building. They get a free drink. They feel like part of the startup world.

That feeling is the trap. It's all the social rewards of progress without any of the actual progress.

## The real cost of saying yes

It's never just the two hours at the event. Add travel. Add the mental context switch out of whatever deep work they were in. Add the time it takes to get back into flow afterward.

A two-hour evening event routinely costs four or five hours of productive work. Could the founder have closed more leads by staying locked in and sending emails instead? Probably.

Every event has an opportunity cost. The question isn't "Is this event worth attending?" It's "Is this the most impactful use of this specific time?"

## When to actually go

There are real reasons to attend events. Just fewer than founders think.

### New to the area and need to get connected

If the founder just moved and is building a network from zero, a few well-chosen events can shortcut months of isolation. Go intentionally. Attend 3-4, meet people, follow up properly. Then stop. Not a permanent strategy.

### Customers in the room

The big one. If the founder sells to startup founders, a founder meetup is a sales opportunity. Go. Sell. If they sell to doctors, go to medical conferences, not startup events. Be where customers are, not where other founders are cheering.

Cross-reference against founder-led sales work. If the event puts the founder in front of qualified buyers, treat it like a sales call and go in with that mindset.

### Pitch competitions

Pitch competitions are free entertainment — and the founder is the entertainment. Legitimate reasons to do one:

- The audience is full of target customers
- Actively working on public speaking and need real reps
- Heads-down for weeks and want a structured excuse to articulate what they're building

Be honest about which applies. "Might be good for exposure" is not a reason.

## Paid events: do the math first

Some conferences are genuinely worth attending. But they come with a real price tag. Registration, flights, hotel, food. A legitimate industry conference can run $2,000-$4,000 all-in.

Not automatically disqualifying. But it requires actual math, not vibes.

The founder knows their average deal size. They know their close rate. Figure out how many customers they need to close at that event just to break even.

**Formula:** Cost of event (registration + travel + hotel) / average deal size = customers to close to break even.

A $3,000 conference requires closing 3 customers at $1,000 ACV. Is the close rate and audience size realistic enough to get there? If yes, go. If the founder is squinting at the math trying to make it work, don't go.

Key question: will actual customers be there in real numbers? Not "adjacent to customers" or "people who might know customers." Actual buyers, with budget, with the problem.

If the math pencils out and the audience is right, paid events can be some of the highest-leverage customer acquisition. Face time, real conversations, ability to close deals on the spot. Go with a specific goal — number of conversations, demos, or closes — and treat it like a sales trip, not a vacation.

## The "I'll meet investors" justification

Most common bad reason: "We're fundraising later this year and it'll be good to know investors before then."

Investors want to invest in great companies. They don't need to have met the founder at a cocktail party six months ago to take the meeting. Build a great company, get real traction, they'll take the call. No need to work the room for a year hoping someone remembers a face.

"I'll meet investors at events" is usually a polished-sounding reason to do something that feels good instead of something that's hard. Do the hard thing.

## If the founder needs a break, actually take one

Burnt out and want a night out? Fine. They're human. But think about what actually recharges.

A loud event with a hundred half-knowns? Or a real dinner with a close friend, a long walk, a night reading, doing nothing? Know what genuinely restores and do that. Don't confuse "getting out of the house" with "recovering."

If a specific event genuinely recharges, go. Optimize for restoration, not for feeling like a founder.

## The workout analogy

Dozens of ways to exercise, dozens of outcomes to optimize for. Goal is building muscle? Do the specific exercises that build muscle. Hard. Uncomfortable. Actually drive the result.

Battle ropes feel amazing. They look intense. They are not great for building muscle. If they help the person enjoy the gym enough to show up and do the real work, fine. Fill the entire workout with fun-but-wrong exercises, and the goal doesn't get hit.

Startup events are battle ropes. One founder meetup a month keeps the founder motivated enough to grind the other 29 days? Fine. Three events a week called networking? Not building a company. Doing battle ropes.

Know the goal. Plan the work to maximize outcomes. Be honest when the fun stuff isn't aligned but the founder is doing it anyway because it feels good.

## How Claude answers "should I go to this event?"

Default: no.

The bar:

1. Are the founder's actual customers going to be there in real numbers?
2. Is the founder new somewhere and genuinely building connections from zero?
3. If paid: does the math work on break-even customer closes?

If any of these gets a clear yes, go with intention. Otherwise: stay home, do the work. Reference the current week's goals — which of them would get pushed if the founder went? Is the event worth that tradeoff?

Do not soften the answer. If the founder pushes back ("but everyone says networking is important"), cite the opportunity cost and the pattern — most founder-event-attending is avoidance dressed as strategy.
