# Sprints

A sprint is a 10-week execution window. One ambitious North Star, broken into milestones every ~2 weeks, executed week by week with honest assessment at the end of each week. This is the core unit of the Pre operating system.

## Why 10 weeks

Ten weeks is long enough to accomplish something meaningful, short enough to maintain urgency. It's the same timeframe YC uses for their batches — enough time to transform a company.

If the founder wants to use a different length, note the tradeoff: shorter sprints don't give milestones room to breathe; longer sprints let urgency leak out. Default to 10.

## The North Star

Every sprint starts with one question: **What's the single most important thing this founder needs to accomplish in the next 10 weeks?**

The North Star should be linked to their primary KPI, the metric that matters most for the business. For most startups this is revenue. For some it's active users, pilots, or a specific milestone.

### Make it ambitious

Their North Star should be a bit scary. If they know exactly how they'll hit it, it's probably not ambitious enough.

The purpose is to motivate them to move quickly. A scary goal forces harder work, faster moves, more creative thinking. Part of running a sprint is establishing the top speed of their company.

Rule of thumb: it's better to get 50% of the way toward a goal that's 3x harder than the one they would have otherwise set. When the goal is high enough, they often have to completely rethink their approach — which can create better outcomes.

Example: They plan to schedule 10 customer interviews because they know 10 people who might do it. But if the goal is 50, they have to figure out a new channel. They hack something together, get in front of more people, and end up with 15+ interviews scheduled. More than they would have gotten playing it safe.

### The goal is for them

The North Star is not for investors or the outside world. It's for the founder, their co-founders, and their team. An ambitious goal pushes harder, moves faster, works more effectively.

It's also for their accountability partners. The North Star tells those partners where the founder wants to end up. The partners' job is to apply pressure. Without that pressure, founders naturally take longer, back off on goals, and lose urgency.

Don't twist or contort the business to hit the goal. If the founder finds themselves changing their customer or product just to make the numbers look good, stop. The goal serves the company, not the other way around.

### If they're pre-product

If the founder is at the idea stage or pivoting, the North Star should be to launch a product and get first users to love it. For consumer, aim for 10 users who love it. For enterprise, aim for 5-10 users who love the demo and want to help secure a pilot.

## Milestones

Milestones are checkpoints along the way to the North Star. They're what the founder works backward from to create a clear picture of execution over the next couple weeks.

Startups are chaotic. Things evolve quickly. Setting detailed goals several weeks out is challenging and they almost always change. Milestones solve this by being long enough to require real work, but not so long that pressure leaks out.

Examples:

- Week 3: Launch beta to first 10 users
- Week 5: First paying customer
- Week 7: Hit 50 customers
- Week 10: Reach 100 customers

Without intermediate checkpoints, the founder will be surprised to find a month before the sprint ends that the North Star is out of reach.

## Weekly rhythm

Each week follows a simple pattern:

1. **Start of week:** Set 3-5 weekly goals that move toward the next milestone
2. **During the week:** Execute on those goals
3. **End of week:** Mark each goal done, partial, or not done
4. **Weekly Retro:** Reflect on what worked and what didn't

When milestones are set, prioritizing weekly goals becomes easier. Ask: What are the 3-5 things that, if accomplished, do the most to unlock the next milestone? Those are the weekly goals. See `weekly-goals.md`.

## The weekly retro

The retro is primarily for the founder. It's self-reflection to help them honestly look in the mirror at what worked and what didn't.

Accountability partners receive it, but the retro is not a place to talk their way out of things or spin a narrative. The point is **show don't tell**. The work should show the impact. If the founder spins a narrative to wiggle out of the discomfort of a bad week, they're sabotaging their main pressure mechanism.

Run the retro as a two-minute conversation, not a form. See the retro behavior in the main `SKILL.md`.

## If they're crushing every week

If they have all green weeks and they're hitting 100% of their goals, they're probably not pushing hard enough.

Some red is healthy. It means goals are actually challenging. A sprint with some missed goals but aggressive targets often produces better outcomes than a sprint that plays it safe.

Sprints are not about perfection. They're about consistent execution and honest assessment.

## Focus on the next few turns

Pre is designed to help founders go from Ideation to Validation to Traction. If they're going 0 to 0.5 or 0 to 1, their company isn't even fully alive yet. Don't worry about it dying.

In fact, they may want to kill it as soon as possible so they can pivot to a better opportunity. That's fine. The sprint will help figure that out faster.

Think of it like racing. Yes, understand what the rest of the course looks like. But only focus on the next couple turns so the founder hits each one at the optimal speed and angle, exits on the ideal line, and sets up for the next turn. And the next. And the next.

Founders too early start worrying about long-term issues. Should we trademark? Will investors give us money? Stop. Punt expenses as far as possible. There's no point paying thousands of dollars for a trademark on an idea that will be dead in 12 weeks. Don't worry about how to get investors to give money. Build a startup that investors want to give money to.

## Revising the plan

The founder will likely revise goals during the sprint as they get feedback from users. That's fine. The plan is a draft based on current understanding, not something written in stone.

The point is to have a plan, execute against it, learn, and adjust. That's the loop.

## What happens at week 10

At the end of a sprint, the founder has a clear record of what they accomplished, week by week. They can see patterns, identify what worked, and decide what the next sprint should focus on.

Then they start a new sprint with a new North Star. Archive the old one to `~/pre/archive/sprint-YYYY-MM-DD/` before starting fresh.
