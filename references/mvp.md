# Your First MVP

The founder has talked to customers and heard their problems. Now they want to build. This is where most founders waste months and thousands of dollars building something nobody asked for.

**Stop.** Before writing code, hiring a contractor, or spending money on anything.

## Validation narrows choices

At the end of real validation, things should feel smaller, not bigger.

The founder should know:

- Who this is **not** for
- Which use cases matter
- Which features do **not** matter
- What they already pay for
- Why they switch (or don't)

If everything still feels possible, they haven't validated anything. Go back and do more customer interviews.

## Find the one thing

Interviews should reveal a core problem. Not five problems. Not a platform. One problem painful enough that someone will pay to make it go away.

The MVP is not a list of features. It's the smallest thing that solves that one problem well enough to test whether people will actually use it and pay for it.

If the founder can't describe their MVP in one sentence, it's too big. Format: "We help [specific person] do [specific thing] without [specific pain]."

## First customers don't need a product

The founder doesn't need software to validate demand. They can:

- Do it manually
- Use email, Notion, spreadsheets, Slack, text messages
- Act as the product themselves

If someone won't accept the solution when it's manual and ugly, they're not a customer. They're an audience member.

The goal of an MVP is not to impress anyone. It's to learn whether people will use this when it exists. Pretty comes later. Working comes first.

## The concierge test

Before building anything, try doing the job manually. For real customers.

If the product is a scheduling tool, be the scheduling tool. Email people, coordinate calendars, send reminders. Do it by hand for 10 customers.

The founder will learn more in one week of manual work than in three months of building. They'll discover edge cases, workflow problems, and whether people actually care enough to use this thing.

## Do not hire a contractor

Founders love to think they can shortcut the work by paying someone else to build it. This almost never works at the MVP stage.

Why:

- The founder doesn't know what to build yet. They'd be paying someone to build their guesses.
- Every change costs money and time. Iteration needs to be fast.
- Contractors build what's spec'd, not what's needed. The founder doesn't know what's needed yet.
- Time will go into managing the contractor instead of learning from customers.

If there's $10k to spend, don't spend it on development. Spend it on time. Give 10 more weeks to manually test the idea before writing code.

## Build the ugliest thing that works

When the founder does build, build the minimum. Not the "minimum viable product" that looks like a real product. The actual minimum.

- No onboarding flow. Just a form.
- No dashboard. Just an email with results.
- No mobile app. Just a website that works on mobile.
- No payment integration. Just send an invoice.

Every feature added is a feature to maintain, explain, and potentially remove later. Start with less than seems possible.

## Speed over polish

The MVP should take days or weeks, not months. If it's taking months, they're building too much.

The goal is to get something in front of real users as fast as possible so they can learn. Every day spent building is a day not learning from customers.

Set a deadline. "I will have something testable in 2 weeks." Then cut scope ruthlessly to hit it. Whatever gets cut probably wasn't needed anyway.

## Is it working?

**Working if:**

- People use it without being asked to
- They come back without reminders
- They tell other people about it
- They ask when they can pay (or they already are)
- They complain when it breaks

**Not working if:**

- The founder has to convince people to try it
- They try it once and disappear
- They say "this is cool" but don't use it
- They have no feedback because they haven't actually used it

## When to build more

The founder earns the right to build more features when:

1. There are users who actively use what's built
2. Those users are asking for specific improvements
3. The founder understands *why* they want those improvements
4. Adding the feature will help retain or grow users

Until then, keep it small. The best founders resist the urge to build. They stay close to customers and let demand pull features out of them.

Building feels like progress. It's not. Customers using the product is progress. Revenue is progress. Everything else is theater.
