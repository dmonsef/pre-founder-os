# Customer Validation

The #1 reason startups fail is building something nobody wants. Customer validation is how you avoid that fate. It's not optional. It's the most important work before writing a single line of code.

## Always start here

Founders build because building feels like progress. It's not. It's insulation from reality.

**Do not skip validation.**
**Do not build it yet.**
**Do not spend money yet.**

Common excuses for skipping validation:

**"The market is already validated."** Market validation only means someone, somewhere, wants something like this. It doesn't validate the specific approach, wedge, distribution, or ability to win. A validated market with no traction is often worse than an unvalidated one, because it removes excuses.

**"I've already built most of it."** Building is not progress unless customers are asking for specific improvements. Shipping code only matters if it creates pull. If the founder built a lot and learned little, that's a negative signal, not an asset.

**"I've been working on this for months or years."** Time spent is not evidence. Conviction doesn't matter. In startups, a long time without sharp signal is counter-evidence.

**"But my building was validating because..."** If the building was truly validating, the founder wouldn't need to argue for it. The results would argue for them.

If the founder skipped validation, the best thing they can do is pause product work and focus on real validation. Past-them is not the concern. Future-them is.

## Why it matters

Most founders skip validation because they're excited to build. They assume they understand the problem because they've experienced it themselves. This is a trap.

Their experience is not representative. Their friends are too polite. Their mom thinks everything they do is brilliant. None of this is validation.

Real validation comes from strangers who have no reason to spare feelings — and who demonstrate their interest by taking action, not just saying nice things.

## The three questions

The goal of early customer validation is to answer three questions:

1. **Does the problem exist?** Are people actually experiencing the pain?
2. **Is it painful enough?** Do they care enough to pay for a solution, or is it just a minor annoyance?
3. **Are they actively looking for solutions?** If they're not already trying to solve it, they probably won't pay someone else to.

## The Mom Test

The best framework for customer interviews comes from Rob Fitzpatrick's book "The Mom Test." Core insight: don't ask people if the idea is good. Ask about their life, their problems, and their existing behavior.

**Bad:** "Would you use an app that helps you track expenses?"
**Good:** "How do you currently track expenses? Walk me through the last time you did it."

Questions that actually work:

- "Tell me about the last time you experienced [problem]."
- "What have you tried to solve this?"
- "What don't you like about your current solution?"
- "How much time or money does this problem cost you?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand, what would the ideal solution look like?"

See `interviews.md` for the full interview protocol.

## How many interviews

No magic number, but a rule of thumb: keep interviewing until the founder stops learning new things. Usually 10-20 conversations.

If every interview teaches something completely new, they haven't done enough. If they can predict what someone will say before they say it, probably done enough.

## Signs of validation

**Strong signals:**

- People ask how they can sign up or buy
- They offer to pay before there's a product
- They introduce the founder to others with the same problem
- They've already spent money trying to solve it
- They get emotional when describing the problem

**Weak signals:**

- "That sounds cool" or "I'd probably use that"
- Generic positive feedback
- Feature suggestions without expressing real pain
- "My friend would love this"

**Red flag:** If people won't give their email, schedule a follow-up call, or take any small action — they're not as interested as they're saying. Polite interest is not validation.

## Common mistakes

**Only talking to friends and family.** The founder's network will tell them what they want to hear. Cold outreach to strangers is uncomfortable but essential.

**Leading the witness.** "Don't you think it's annoying when...?" is not a real question. It's asking them to agree.

**Pitching instead of listening.** Interviews are for learning, not selling. If the founder is talking more than 20% of the time, they're doing it wrong.

**Stopping too early.** Five interviews is not enough. Neither is ten if they're all with the same type of person. Seek diversity.

## What comes next

Once there's validation that the problem exists and people want a solution, the founder can think about what to build. But even then, start small. Build the smallest thing that lets them learn whether the solution actually works. See `mvp.md`.

Validation is never "done." Throughout the life of the company, the founders who win stay close to their users.
