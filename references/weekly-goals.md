# Weekly Goals

Weekly goals are the engine of the sprint. Get them right and the founder makes consistent progress. Get them wrong and they spin their wheels.

## Goals, not tasks

A weekly goal is an outcome, not a task. It's something you can definitively say was accomplished or not.

- **Task:** "Work on landing page"
- **Goal:** "Launch landing page and get 50 email signups"

- **Task:** "Do customer interviews"
- **Goal:** "Complete 10 customer interviews and document key insights"

If you can't clearly mark it done or not done at the end of the week, it's not a good goal.

## Clearly measurable

Good goals are clearly measurable. At minimum, pass/fail: either they shipped it or they didn't.

But the best goals have a target number. Numbers remove ambiguity and force honest assessment.

- **Vague:** "Start working on articles for the handbook"
- **Measurable:** "Get 5 articles published in the handbook"

The vague goal is technically "done" the moment they type the first letter. The measurable goal has a clear finish line.

When a target number is set, the founder knows exactly where they stand at the end of the week. Did they hit 5? Did they hit 3? No wiggle room.

## The right number of goals

3-5 goals per week. More than that and they're spreading too thin. Fewer than that and they're probably not being ambitious enough.

## Connected to milestones

Weekly goals should drive toward unlocking the next milestone. Milestones push toward the North Star.

Ask: "What are the 3-5 things that, if accomplished this week, do the most to unlock your next milestone?"

If the founder can't draw a clear line between a goal and the upcoming milestone, question whether it belongs this week. Nice-to-haves can wait. The sprint is for must-haves.

## The goal audit

This is the single most important behavior Claude performs in this skill.

When the founder proposes a goal that is vague, unmeasurable, task-shaped (not outcome-shaped), not in their control, or disconnected from the next milestone: flag it, explain the problem in one sentence, and offer three sharper rewrites labeled a, b, c.

Show, don't just tell. Example:

> Your goal: "Make progress on the pitch deck"
>
> That's a task, not an outcome. "Progress" has no finish line. Three sharper versions:
>
> a. "Finish pitch deck v1 and get it reviewed by 2 founders"
> b. "Ship pitch deck and use it in 5 investor meetings"
> c. "Ship pitch deck + warm-intro list of 20 target investors"
>
> Pick one, ask for different options, or keep what you wrote.

The three options should be genuinely different — not minor rewordings of the same thing. They should reflect different levels of ambition or different outcomes the founder might be aiming at.

Common failure modes to audit on:

- **Too vague**: "Make progress on product" → put a number on it.
- **Not in their control**: "Get featured in TechCrunch" depends on others. "Pitch 10 journalists" is in their control.
- **Too many**: If there are 8+ goals, there are no priorities. Cut.
- **Disconnected from milestone**: If it doesn't push toward the next checkpoint, it probably shouldn't be this week's goal.

Do not skip the audit to be agreeable. A founder writing sharper goals for three weeks is worth more than most other advice.

## Honest assessment

At the end of each week, mark each goal honestly:

- **Done:** Accomplished what was set out
- **Partial:** Made progress but didn't fully hit the target
- **Not Done:** Didn't get to it or couldn't finish

If a goal had a target number and it's partial, enter the number actually accomplished. "Get 5 customer interviews" marked partial with 3 is useful data. It tells you exactly how close.

Marking something "not done" isn't failure. It's data. It tells you something about planning, priorities, or obstacles.

## The weekly retro

Where the founder processes how they prioritized their goals for the last week. What worked? What didn't? What got in the way?

This reflection is what helps them set better, higher-impact goals for the week ahead. Without it, they keep making the same planning mistakes.
