# Avoid Premature Optimization

A super common mistake early-stage founders make: spending precious time and energy on problems they haven't earned the right to have yet.

**Don't try to get into YC.** Build a company YC wants to accept.
**Don't try to raise a round.** Build a company investors want to fund.
**Don't plan for scale.** Build something people actually want first.

## The trap of future problems

Founders love to think about the future. It's exciting. Having a vision for the future is often what inspires us. But spending energy on future problems before solving present ones is a form of procrastination disguised as productivity.

Common premature optimizations:

- **Getting into YC** before there's traction worth accepting
- **Fundraising strategy** before there's a business worth funding
- **Acquisition planning** before there's anything worth acquiring
- **Scaling operations** before doing it manually 100+ times
- **Hiring plans** before being truly bottlenecked by a lack of hours
- **Legal structure optimization** before validating there's a company to form

Every hour spent on these future problems is an hour not spent on the thing that actually unlocks them: building something people want.

## Build something people want

YC's motto isn't just a nice slogan. It's the entire game at the early stage.

"Want" isn't ephemeral. It's measurable. Real want shows up as:

- Users who come back without being reminded
- Customers who pay money
- People who tell their friends
- Engagement that grows week over week

Without these signals, there's no product-market fit. And without PMF, nothing else matters. Not the pitch deck. Not the cap table. Not the YC application.

## The investor trap in secondary markets

In secondary startup ecosystems (cities where most angel investors made their money in real estate, medicine, or law rather than tech), founders face a unique challenge: investors who don't understand how early-stage startups work.

These investors often ask for:

- 3-5 year financial projections
- Detailed pro formas
- Complex business plans
- ROI calculations based on assumptions

For a pre-seed startup, detailed 3-year financial projections are fiction. The founder is going to get punched in the face by reality every week. The plan will change. The question is whether they can adapt, not whether the spreadsheet was accurate.

Mike Tyson said it best: "Everyone has a plan until they get punched in the face." In startups, that happens every day.

A plan is useful. Seeing where the business could go in 3+ years is useful. A rough outline of the next 18 months covering go-to-market, product roadmap, revenue, and expenses is useful. But that plan is a living document that evolves weekly, not a promise carved in stone.

Founders who spend weeks perfecting pro formas for non-tech investors are often using that work to avoid the harder, scarier work: talking to customers and building an MVP.

## The scale fantasy

Another trap: founders in early markets spend enormous energy worrying about future scale. Future exits. Future outcomes.

"What happens when we have 10,000 users?"
"How will we handle enterprise customers?"
"What's our exit strategy?"

Great problems to have. But the founder doesn't have them yet. Planning for them now is like drawing a house with closed eyes — might get shapes roughly right, nothing will be where it needs to be.

Build a company that has the *opportunity* to scale. When it gets there, the founder will know exactly what's needed because they'll be living it.

## Do things that don't scale

Paul Graham's essay is holy scripture for early-stage founders. Core insight: manual, unscalable work is how you learn what actually needs to be built.

This applies to everything:

- **Customer support:** Do it yourself before building a help desk
- **Sales:** Close deals manually before building a sales team
- **Operations:** Do it by hand before automating
- **Onboarding:** Walk users through it personally before building flows

When the founder does things manually, they learn exactly how they work. They discover edge cases. They understand what actually matters vs. what they assumed would matter. Then — and only then — they optimize, operationalize, and automate.

## Learn by doing

Startups are not theoretical exercises. You cannot think your way to product-market fit. You cannot plan your way to traction. You have to do the work.

Every customer conversation teaches something. Every manual process reveals inefficiencies. Every failure shows what to fix. This is the iterative loop:

1. Do the thing manually
2. Learn what works and what doesn't
3. Iterate and improve
4. Repeat until you deeply understand the process
5. Only then: optimize, systematize, automate

The founders who win are not the ones with the best plans. They're the ones who execute fastest and learn the most from each iteration.

## What to focus on instead

If pre-PMF, the entire focus should be:

1. **Talking to customers** — understand pain deeply
2. **Building an MVP** — the smallest thing that tests the hypothesis
3. **Getting users** — manually, one at a time
4. **Learning and iterating** — weekly cycles

That's it. Everything else is a distraction until there's evidence that people want what's being built.

## When it's time to think bigger

The founder earns the right to think about scale, fundraising, and exits when:

- Users love the product (not just use it)
- There's revenue (or a clear path to revenue)
- Growth is straining the current approach
- They've learned enough to know what's actually needed

When the founder gets there, they won't need to force it. Investors will find them. Scale problems will present themselves. The path forward will be obvious because they'll have earned the knowledge to see it.

## How Claude uses this

When the founder brings up future-flavored work (fundraising, YC applications, scaling plans, hiring strategies, legal structures, exit strategies), check their current phase from `~/pre/founder.md` first.

If they're pre-PMF and asking about Series A strategy, redirect kindly but firmly. Ask what would actually unlock the next turn in their race. Reference the milestone they're trying to hit this sprint.

If they genuinely have earned the right to think bigger (users love it, revenue, growth pressure), engage with the question. But verify first — don't take their word for it if there's connector data that can confirm.
