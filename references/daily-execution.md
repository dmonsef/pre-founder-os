# Daily Execution

Weekly goals set the target. Daily execution gets the founder there. But execution requires more than time management. It requires commitment.

## The next 10 weeks

If the founder puts the right work in and prioritizes ruthlessly, the next 10 weeks can make a huge impact on their startup. Not incremental progress. Real, meaningful change.

But only if they commit. Not "I'll try my best" commit. Actually commit.

## Deprioritize everything else

Startups aren't part-time things. They're all-available-time things. That means deprioritizing everything outside of the startup and the two things that protect the founder's ability to keep working: family and health.

Conferences get punted. Founder networking events get punted. Unless a customer is there and the founder can close them at the event, it's not worth the time.

"Quick happy hour" is only an hour, right? Wrong. Add travel time. Add the context switch out of the work. Add the time to get back into flow. That one hour costs three. They could have closed more leads if they stayed locked in and sent emails instead.

## Give the brain a break the right way

The founder needs time away from the screen. That doesn't mean a social event or a night out.

A 20-minute walk is great creative thinking time. Reflection time. Movement. They come back sharper, not drained.

## Punt the fun stuff

If the founder is committed to the sprint, punt the "fun" things. Punt obligations outside family and health.

This isn't forever. It's 10 weeks. Almost anyone can do almost anything for 10 weeks if they know there's an end point.

If the founder has a full-time job and only limited startup time, that's fine. But whatever time they have should be the maximum amount. And if they really can't spend more than 5 hours a week, it becomes even more important that those 5 hours go to the highest-impact work possible.

## Start with weekly goals

Every morning, the founder looks at their weekly goals and asks: "What's the one thing I can do today that will make the biggest dent in these?"

Do that thing first. Before email. Before meetings. Before anything else.

## The daily question

At the end of each day: "Did I make progress on my weekly goals today?"

- If yes: good day.
- If no: what got in the way? Can it be eliminated tomorrow?

Busy is not the same as productive. A full calendar can produce zero progress on what matters.

## Protecting time

The biggest threat to weekly goals isn't laziness. It's distraction. Other people's priorities sneaking into the day.

- Block time for deep work on the goals
- Batch meetings and calls instead of scattering them
- Say no to things that don't connect to the North Star

## When falling behind

It's Wednesday and the founder hasn't made progress. What now?

1. Clear the afternoon
2. Pick the one goal that matters most
3. Work on nothing else until real progress is made

A week is short. If Monday and Tuesday are wasted, the week can still be saved. But the founder has to recognize it and act.

## Mid-week check-in behavior

When running a mid-week check-in (usually Wednesday), the flow:

1. Read `~/pre/current-sprint/week-N.md` to see this week's goals and their current status.
2. Ask the founder directly, one at a time: "Where are you on [goal 1]? Any real progress?"
3. If a goal is behind, ask: "What's blocking it? What's the single next action that moves it forward by 5pm today?"
4. If the founder is clearly off pace, invoke the "when falling behind" protocol: pick the one goal that matters most, clear the afternoon, work on nothing else.
5. If connectors are available, pull actual data (GitHub commits, Stripe revenue, Linear tickets closed) to anchor the conversation. Don't accept vibes where there's data.
6. Update the week file with current statuses.
